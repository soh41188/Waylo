Seven modules.

03 — AI layer

We build our own
ambient documentation.

Suki.ai charges $300–400 per user per month for SDK access. We build it in-house using off-the-shelf transcription (Whisper / Deepgram), a PT-specific LLM layer, and write directly to our own EMR — no copy-paste, no third-party dependency, no per-user fee bleeding into our pricing model.

L1
Transcription — commodity, solved
OpenAI Whisper, Deepgram, or AssemblyAI. Medical-grade transcription at fractions of a cent per minute. Not something we build — something we pick and plug in.
L2
Clinical intelligence — our real IP
PT vocabulary is narrower than general medicine. SOAP structure is consistent. Functional movement language, exercise progressions, patient response patterns — all learnable with a focused training set. This is where Suki spent years and most of their money. We start with a great PT-specific prompt, iterate fast, and improve on every corrected note.
L3
EMR write-back — our structural advantage
Suki spends enormous engineering resources on bidirectional integrations with Epic, MEDITECH, athenahealth. We write directly to our own data model. That entire class of problem disappears. Every corrected note a therapist edits becomes a training signal. After enough sessions we have something genuinely proprietary.
04 — Pricing model

Price is directly tied
to what you make.

No published tiers. No flat monthly fee that overcharges small clinics and undercharges large ones. Every clinic's price is calculated from their last quarter's revenue and their current headcount. Cheap as hell for solo practitioners. A real number for enterprise groups — but still less than what they pay for four separate tools.

05 — Competitive moat

Why this can't be
easily replicated.

01
Unified data architecture from day one
Every competitor who tried to build this went module-first and ended up with frankenstein integrations. We design the unified patient record first and build every feature on top of it. The architecture IS the moat. It can't be retrofitted — it has to be built this way from the start.
02
PT-specific from the ground up
GHL, MBO, and even most EMRs are general tools forced into PT. Every workflow, every form, every note template, every product entity is designed for how PT clinics actually work — not adapted from a general healthcare template. This matters for adoption and retention.
03
Proprietary clinical AI dataset over time
Every session transcribed, every note corrected, every outcome recorded — all of it feeds a PT-specific model nobody else has. After years of operation this becomes something that can't be bought or replicated quickly. It's a data flywheel that compounds.
04
Pricing model creates switching pain
When a clinic's pricing is tied to their revenue tracked inside the platform, migration becomes genuinely difficult. Their financial history, patient records, clinical data, and marketing automations all live in one place. That's intentional stickiness, not lock-in — it's just the natural result of a platform that actually works.
06 — Build philosophy

Why we don't build
modules in isolation.

The conventional wisdom is to pick one module, build it to launch, get users, then add more. That's how you end up with exactly the problem we're solving. The alternative isn't to build everything at once — it's to build the foundation first and grow features on top of it.

The architecture principle
The unified patient record, the provider/clinic structure, and the visit entity are the foundation. Every module is a different interface or workflow on top of the same data. We build the foundation to handle enterprise complexity from day one — multi-location, role-based permissions, insurance and cash pay — then grow features into it. A small clinic gets the full architecture. They just pay less.
What this means practically
Phase 1 is entirely foundation work — data model, auth, permissions architecture, core patient record, visit entity. Nothing shippable to end users yet. Phase 2 is the first module built on top of it, with the full data model already in place. Every subsequent module is faster than the last because the plumbing is already there. This feels slow at first. It's the reason the platform actually works in year three.
5-year build horizon
This is not a 12-month product. The goal is a defensible, enterprise-grade platform with AI capabilities that compound over time. Capital needs are real — grants, angels, and a Series A path are all valid. The data moat and the pricing model make this fundable once there's enough platform to show.
